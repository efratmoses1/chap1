Introduction to the Hebrew language

Hebrew is a Semitic language native to Israel and consists of biblical Hebrew and Modern Hebrew. Modern Hebrew is an official language of Israel and is based on Biblical Hebrew along with modern additions. Biblical Hebrew is used for prayer and liturgy studies. Historically it is regarded as the language of the Israelites during ancient times and is the language in which the Hebrew Bible was written.

Hebrew had ceased to be an everyday spoken language about two thousand years ago. During the medieval period, it was the language of Jewish liturgy, rabbinic literature, prayers, and poetry. In the 19th century, Eliezer Ben-Yehuda began reviving Hebrew as a modern spoken language. He created many new vocabulary words that reflected the modern world and insisted on using it as an everyday spoken language. Eventually, as a result of a local language movement he created, but more significantly as a result of a new groups of Jewish immigrants returning to their homeland, it replaced a score of languages spoken by Jews at that time. Today Modern Hebrew is spoken by more than nine million people.

Some key features of the Hebrew language are:

·        Type of writing system: abjad (A type of writing system where each symbol usually stands for consonant, leaving the reader to supply the appropriate vowel)

·        Direction of writing: right to left in horizontal lines.

·        Number of letters:22  consonants, plus diacritics and final letters. Some letters (kaf, mem, nun, feh and tzadi) have a final form (sofit), which is used when they appear at the end of a word.

·        The letters stand alone and are not connected even in a cursive form.

·        Used to write: Hebrew, Judeo-Arabic, Ladino, Yiddish and many other Jewish languages.

·        There are no separate numbers in Hebrew, instead standard western numbers (1, 2, 3, etc.) are used.
